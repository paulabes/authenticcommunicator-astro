# Is using your hands when you speak distracting? Actually, no.

Last Friday I had the absolute pleasure of working with a wonderful group of diverse delegates from around the world.

One participant had noticed that in the UK, stillness seems to be the default. Hands clasped. Gesture minimal. She wondered whether keeping her hands still might actually be the more professional choice.

## Why British professional culture defaults to stillness

British professional culture does tend towards tension. Hands in pockets, hands clasped, bodies held very still - it reads as controlled and composed from the inside.

The problem is how it reads from the outside. What feels composed to the speaker lands as flat to the room.

## What happens in the room when you hold your body still

When we hold our bodies completely still, we strip ourselves of the power to connect. The listener is given nothing to follow. Our voices go flat. There is no natural emphasis, no rhythm, no shape - and no sense that we ourselves care about what we're saying.

That is a high price to pay for "professionalism".

## What gesture actually does for your audience

Gesture isn't distraction. It tells the listener where to look, what matters, and when to engage. When you bring your whole body into how you speak, you stop *managing* the words and start actually *expressing* them.

Good gesture does three things at once:

- It directs attention, so the audience knows which idea is the point
- It unlocks your voice, because tone, pitch, and pace move with the body
- It signals conviction, because stillness reads as detachment and movement reads as care

## The experiment that changes people's minds

We ran a small experiment in the workshop. She delivered her personal introduction twice. Once with complete stillness. Once with gesture that matched the meaning.

The difference was worlds apart. Same words, same speaker, completely different room.

Stillness isn't professionalism. It's flat. It fails to connect.

If you're still not convinced, watch your favourite speaker. I guarantee they are not still.
