# How I made my Grade 5 piano exam a life-or-death scenario

My friend Sarah told me to write this. Shout out to Sarah Moule - actor, singer, and all-round superhuman. I didn't want to write it, because honestly, it's humiliating. But as I've sat with it, I've realised it might help you. Yes, you, the one reading this who is facing some kind of performance anxiety.

So I'm doing this for you. Please be grateful. Because this is painful for me.

## Why performance anxiety shows up even for experienced performers

You're probably feeling that anxiety because you're up against a situation you only get one chance to get right. Maybe it's a speech, a concert, a show, a match, a presentation, a dance. Something where you'll be observed, judged, stared at, assessed, or simply *perceived*.

As the event creeps closer, you might be starting to feel a bit sick. You might be muttering things like, "Oh God, why did I sign up for this?" or "How can I get out of this thing?"

If so, you're very much like me a few months ago, leading up to my ABRSM Grade 5 piano exam.

## The credentials that should have made this easy

If you know me, you'll know I'm a former opera singer and professional musician. I studied at the [Guildhall School of Music and Drama](https://www.gsmd.ac.uk/). I've sung at Glyndebourne and across Europe. I've worked for years as a singing teacher, accompanying students on the piano.

So even admitting that I recently took Grade 5 piano feels deeply embarrassing. Grade 5 is what we did as kids, right? This should be easy for me. Turns out - no.

It's really hard to master a new instrument as an adult. The mental clutter is overwhelming and the self-recriminations relentless. My fingers were stiff and reluctant. My scales - clunky. It was genuinely challenging. And yet I persisted.

## Why imposter syndrome almost sabotaged the exam

The sabotage came from an unexpected place: my other career, helping people with public speaking. A big part of my work is guiding people through performance anxiety. I know the tools. I've lived them. I've taught them. I believe in them.

And yet, a terrifying thought crept in: *what if I use the strategies I teach and they don't work?* If I fail, I'm a fraud. A snake-oil merchant. Everything I've taught is worthless.

So in the run-up to the exam, I had two heavy fears pressing down:

- If I can't do this, I'm not a real musician
- If I can't do this, I'm a fraud, and I can't help anyone

Lovely, huh? Imagine trying to play expressively with those turds on your shoulders.

## What I actually did (the strategies I teach clients)

I knew I had to act, and fast. I decided to draw on everything I'd learned in 20 years of performing and coaching.

The first thing I did was re-read all my own performance anxiety material as if for the first time. No skimming. No half-hearted browsing. I did the work:

- Visualisations, done daily and in detail
- Affirmations, repeated until my body recognised them
- Self-compassion, especially on the days the scales sounded worse
- Expression over perfection - focusing on making music, not avoiding mistakes

It struck me again that you can't just *think* about doing the work. You have to do it. Consistently.

## What happened on the day

I got into performance mode two weeks before the exam. I visualised, hard. I trusted my fingers instead of trying to force them. I softened the panic and let the music come through.

Yes, I was nervous. Yes, I performed well. Yes, I got a good mark. And yes, I proved to myself that the methods I teach actually work - if you commit to them.

## What to take into your own performance

If you're facing your own performance mountain, the invitation is the same. Do the work. Start now. Start with the mental game, because that's what you're really building:

- Trust in yourself
- Trust in your body
- Trust in your preparation
- Trust in your ability

You're building belief. I believe you can do it. Do you?
