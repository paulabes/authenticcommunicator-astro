# How to prepare for a tech fail before a presentation

If you've ever had a presentation ruined by dodgy tech, this is for you.

I'm nuts about preparation when it comes to tech. In theatre and opera, nobody messes around: we know that nothing kills the magic of a story like a crackling microphone or a lighting cue that misses its moment. That training has made me extremely careful - dare I say neurotic - about the technical side of any speaking event.

## My two most cringeworthy tech fails

And yet, despite careful prep, here are two major fails I have still faced in recent years:

- YouTube played loud, aggressive ads on every slide of my PowerPoint, despite me being a paid user and testing it multiple times
- My new Mac refused to share my screen for reasons I still do not understand

Both were cringeworthy. I laughed them off and my audiences were understanding, but inside I still thought: *this is not good enough. Somewhere, somehow, my prep could have been better.*

I'm no luddite. I'm a music producer as well as a coach. I understand tech and I'm good at it. And yet I am not so foolish as to trust it.

## My pre-event tech checklist

Here's the checklist I now run before every paid speaking event to reduce the chance of another tech-fail embarrassment:

- A second laptop ready to go - my old near-dead mule who still just about works
- Both laptops checked for updates the day before (Zoom crashing because of an update when the meeting starts - avoided)
- All documents emailed to the audience beforehand
- Printed handouts in the room (I am gen X, we like paper)
- Mobile hotspot tethered and ready
- A BCC email to all delegates pre-drafted in case I need to send a new meeting link mid-session
- Google Drive versions of every document ready to share in the chat
- A physical whiteboard as backup (teacher mode: activated)
- Breakout-room alternatives and a Plan B if those fail too

Neurotic? Maybe. But I like knowing that whatever happens, I have a Plan B.

## The mindset that matters more than the checklist

The thing that actually keeps me calm isn't the checklist, it's a single line I repeat to my tech-fear in the days before an event: *even if your tech fails, you can deliver something excellent.*

Because it's true. Experience has shown me: I will handle it. I will still be excellent.

A polished speaker who loses their slides can still hold a room. A nervous speaker who has their slides can still lose one. The tech is an amplifier, not the performance.

## What to take into your next event

If you've got tech-fail fear, borrow a few of these ideas and tell your story without interruption. Print a version. Rehearse the first five minutes without slides at all. Know what you'd do if the projector died mid-sentence.
