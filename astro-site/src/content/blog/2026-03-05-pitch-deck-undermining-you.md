# Why your pitch deck is undermining your pitch

The fastest way to completely undermine your pitch? Use the same deck you send clients for information as your actual pitch deck.

A deck designed to be read is not the same thing as a deck designed to enhance a performance. Most people use one for both, and most pitches lose their audience because of it.

## What a reference deck does that a pitch deck shouldn't

An information deck exists to be read alone at a desk. It carries detail, fine print, and every caveat a client might want to check later.

Pitch decks built from this template usually do all of the following:

- Include too much detail - the key takeaway and driving message get lost
- Contain dense blocks of tiny text no one in the room can read
- Focus on *telling* rather than inspiring the audience to think
- Lack a clear opening hook and a powerful ending
- Fail to use images that actually enhance the message
- Act as a confusing visual distraction behind the speaker

Each of those is fatal on their own. Combined, they guarantee a flat room.

## What a pitch deck should do instead

A performance deck exists to amplify the speaker, not duplicate them. It sets rhythm, direction, and emotional pace. It gives the audience a single thing to look at per beat, then hands attention back to the person talking.

That means: one idea per slide, images that carry meaning rather than decorate, a hook in the first thirty seconds, and an ending that earns the silence before applause.

## Yes, that means building two decks

Yes, creating two decks takes more work - one for the performance, one for the detailed follow-up. Most teams push back on this the first time they hear it.

But consider the cost of losing your audience in the pitch. And consider the impact of a genuinely powerful performance on your outcomes. For a £500k deal, a second deck is a rounding error. For a keynote to 200 people, it's the difference between being remembered and being forgotten.

## Performance is an underrated competitive advantage

Performance is a massively underrated competitive advantage in business. Most people focus on content. Master communicators focus on delivery.

You already have the content. The question is whether the room sees a leader or a reader.
