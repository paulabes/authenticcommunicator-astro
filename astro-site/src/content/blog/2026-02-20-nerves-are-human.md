# Nerves are human - one client's public speaking breakthrough

"My 19-year-old self wouldn't recognise this version of me. Thank you so much."

That was the message I received last week from a client who had just walked off stage knowing she'd nailed her presentation. She has a bestselling book, tens of thousands of followers, and speaks to thousands of people online every day without a second thought.

But in-person, in a room full of real, staring faces, she had been turning down opportunities for years. She'd messaged me weeks earlier with five words: "I'm scared I'll go blank."

## Why public speaking confidence lags behind career success

Public speaking confidence rarely keeps pace with career competence. The gap between how someone looks from the outside and how they actually feel inside is almost always wider than colleagues imagine.

A fast-growing brand, a packed online calendar, and a public profile can all coexist with a deep fear of the physical room. In-person speaking asks something different of the nervous system. Eye contact. A breath-hold in the audience. The scrape of a chair at the back. The camera never delivers these. The body has to be trained to meet them.

## What we worked on to rebuild trust under pressure

The goal was not fearlessness. The goal was a toolkit she could trust when her body hit alarm mode.

We worked on four things:

- Building a set of speaking skills she could rely on automatically, so she could stop monitoring herself and start actually connecting with the room
- Reminding her nervous system of every similar challenge she had already come through well
- Clarifying a clear, meaningful objective for the event, because fear shrinks when you have something more important to focus on
- Rehearsing until the technical elements felt like second nature

By the week of the event, her preparation had stopped being a firefight. It was a body-level familiarity.

## Why the anticipation is usually worse than the event

The anticipation of public speaking is almost always worse than the event itself. She messaged me afterwards to say she was surprised that the fear never really showed up on the day.

That pattern repeats with almost every client. The nervous system predicts disaster; the event turns out to be ordinary. Once someone has felt that a couple of times in their own body, the dread starts to lose its grip.

The goal isn't always fearlessness. It's learning to let the fear come along for the ride without letting it sabotage us.

## What to take from this if your fear is holding you back

It doesn't matter how visible you are, how many followers you have, or how many times you've done something similar before. Nerves are human. They mean you care. Doubt is human.

But if she can do it, you can too.
