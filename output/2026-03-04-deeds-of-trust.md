# Deeds of Trust: What They Mean for Your Property in 2026

You bought a house with someone. Maybe it was your partner, maybe a friend, maybe family. You split the deposit unevenly. One of you paid 70%, the other 30%. Or one of you owned the property before the relationship started, and now you're adding someone to the deeds. A solicitor mentions a "Deed of Trust" and assures you it's watertight — that it protects your contribution if things go wrong.

Fast-forward to 2026. The relationship has ended. You're facing a property dispute. And now you're discovering that **a Deed of Trust is not the legal safety net you thought it was** — especially if you're married or have children.

**In 2026, UK Deeds of Trust legally define property ownership and are crucial for unmarried couples. However, for married couples, the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 grants courts power to redistribute assets based on needs, potentially overriding a Deed. New rules mandate Non-Court Dispute Resolution, with penalties for non-compliance, and strict electronic court bundle requirements.**

## What You Need to Know

- **Deeds of Trust define beneficial ownership** — who owns what percentage — but are not absolute in divorce cases, where the court can redistribute assets based on needs, contributions, and children's welfare.
- **Married couples face different rules than unmarried couples** — the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 gives judges broad discretion to override a Deed of Trust if fairness requires it.
- **2026 rules push you toward mediation and arbitration** — courts now actively mandate Non-Court Dispute Resolution (NCDR), and failure to engage can result in cost penalties and delays.

## What Is a Deed of Trust?

A Deed of Trust is a legal document that records the beneficial ownership of property when the legal title (the names on the deeds at the Land Registry) doesn't reflect the true financial arrangement. It's most commonly used in two scenarios:

1. **Unequal contributions:** You and your partner buy a house together, but one of you puts in 70% of the deposit and the other 30%. The Deed records that split.
2. **Adding someone to the deeds:** You owned a property before the relationship, and now you're adding your partner's name to the title — but the Deed states they only have a small beneficial share (say, 10%) unless you agree otherwise.

For **unmarried couples**, a Deed of Trust is governed by the Trusts of Land and Appointment of Trustees Act 1996 (TOLATA). This Act allows you to apply to the court to enforce the terms of the Deed if there's a dispute. The court will look at the Deed, your contributions, and any other evidence of your intentions when the property was purchased.

For **married couples**, it's a different story.

## The Hard Truth: Deeds of Trust Are Not Absolute in Divorce

Here's the "Curveball" most people don't see coming: **if you're married, a Deed of Trust is only one factor the court will consider** — and it can be completely overridden.

Under Section 25 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, the Family Court has broad powers to redistribute assets upon divorce based on:

- The needs of both parties (especially housing and income needs)
- The welfare of any children under 18
- The contributions each party made (financial and non-financial, like childcare)
- The standard of living during the marriage
- The age and health of both parties
- The duration of the marriage

This means that even if you have a Deed of Trust stating you own 80% of the property and your spouse owns 20%, **the court can order a 50/50 split** — or any other split — if that's deemed fair based on the above factors. The Deed is evidence of your original intentions, but it's not binding.

### Example: The Stay-at-Home Parent Scenario

You owned a flat before you got married. Your spouse moved in, and you added their name to the deeds with a Deed of Trust stating they have a 10% beneficial interest. You have two children together. Your spouse gave up their career to raise the kids. After 10 years, you divorce.

A traditional solicitor would tell you: "The Deed says 90/10, so that's your starting point." But the reality? The court will likely order a much larger share for your ex-spouse — potentially 40–50% or more — because their needs (housing, income) and their contributions (childcare, homemaking) outweigh the original Deed. The judge is not there to rubber-stamp an old document. The judge is there to achieve a fair outcome.

## The New Reality: NCDR First, Court Second

In March 2026, the Family Court is still grappling with backlogs. Private law children cases are taking an average of 36 weeks to resolve. Financial remedy cases (which include property disputes) have seen a 7% increase in applications compared to the same period in 2024. The system is stretched.

To address this, the Family Procedure Rules (FPR 3.3/3.4) now **actively mandate Non-Court Dispute Resolution (NCDR)** at all stages. This means:

- Before you issue court proceedings, you must consider mediation, arbitration, or another form of NCDR.
- You must file a form outlining your views on NCDR.
- The court can adjourn your case at any stage and order you to attempt NCDR — even if you don't want to.
- **Failure to engage with NCDR can result in cost penalties** — the judge can order you to pay the other side's legal costs if you unreasonably refused to mediate.

## The Practical Problem: Enforcing a Deed of Trust

If you're unmarried and your ex-partner is refusing to honour the Deed of Trust, your route is TOLATA. You apply to the court for an order declaring the beneficial interests in the property and (usually) ordering a sale.

But here's the friction: **the court will not automatically enforce the Deed**. The judge will consider:

- The written terms of the Deed
- Your actual financial contributions (mortgage payments, repairs, bills)
- Any evidence that you both intended something different from what the Deed says
- Whether there are children living in the property (the Children Act 1989 allows an unmarried parent to apply for housing for the children, which can complicate matters)

### Example: The Verbal Agreement Problem

You have a Deed of Trust stating you own 70% and your ex-partner owns 30%. But during the relationship, you both paid the mortgage equally for five years. Your ex-partner argues that the Deed is outdated — that you agreed (verbally) to split ownership 50/50 once they'd "paid in" enough. You have no written record of this agreement.

A traditional solicitor would tell you: "You'll need to gather evidence — bank statements, witness testimony — and prepare for a contested hearing." That hearing will take months, cost thousands, and ultimately depend on whose version the judge believes.

## The 2026 Bundle Problem: Stricter Rules, Higher Costs

If you do end up in court, be aware of Practice Direction 27A, which became effective on 2 March 2026. This directive mandates:

- **Electronic court bundles as the default** — no more lever-arch files
- **Strict page limits:** 350 pages overall, with individual document limits (e.g., 6 pages for a First Appointment position statement, 15 pages for a Final Hearing statement)
- **No unnecessary correspondence:** General solicitor letters and full bank statements are prohibited unless the court grants permission

The risk? A traditional solicitor accustomed to voluminous paper bundles will struggle to cram everything into 350 pages. You may find yourself in a dispute with your own legal team about what to include, racking up unnecessary costs.

## What About Digital Assets?

Here's a new wrinkle for 2026: the Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework (CARF), which came into effect on 1 January 2026. If you or your ex-partner hold cryptocurrency, you must now disclose it to your crypto service provider, who will report transaction data to HMRC.

For financial remedy cases, this means **crypto assets are no longer easy to hide**. If you're claiming a 70% share of the family home based on your contributions, but you've also got £50,000 in Bitcoin that you didn't disclose, expect the court to take a dim view — and potentially penalise you with a reduced share of the property. Failure to disclose crypto holdings can result in penalties of up to £300.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can a Deed of Trust be overturned in court?**  
Yes, especially in divorce cases. The Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 gives the court broad powers to redistribute assets based on needs and fairness, potentially overriding the Deed's terms.

**Do I need a Deed of Trust if I'm unmarried?**  
If you're contributing unequally to a property purchase or adding someone to the deeds, a Deed of Trust is essential. Without it, the law assumes joint beneficial ownership (50/50), regardless of who paid what.

**What if my ex-partner won't agree to sell the property?**  
You can apply to the court under TOLATA for an order declaring the beneficial interests and ordering a sale. The court will consider the Deed, your contributions, and any children's needs before making a decision.

**How long does a TOLATA dispute take?**  
On average, private law cases (which include TOLATA disputes) are taking 36 weeks to resolve in 2026. NCDR — mediation or arbitration — is typically faster, often resolving within 8-12 weeks.

**Can a Deed of Trust protect me if we have children?**  
Partially. If you're married, the children's welfare will often trump the Deed's terms. If you're unmarried, the court can still make housing orders under the Children Act 1989, but these don't grant your ex-partner additional property rights — just the right to live there until the children are older.

**What happens if I didn't get a Deed of Trust when I bought the property?**  
You can still apply to the court for a declaration of your beneficial interest, but you'll need strong evidence of your contributions and intentions. This is harder and more expensive than having a Deed in place from the start.
