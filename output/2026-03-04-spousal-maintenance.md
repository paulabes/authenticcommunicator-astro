# Spousal Maintenance After Divorce: What You Actually Need to Know in 2026

Divorce is never just about the marriage ending — it's about untangling a life. And for many, one of the most pressing questions is: "Will I have to support my ex financially?" or "Will I receive support?" This is spousal maintenance, and it's a topic wrapped in confusion, anxiety, and often, resentment.

You're in the right place. At Parachute Law, we cut through the fog. **Spousal maintenance in the UK is financial support paid by one ex-spouse to another after divorce, determined by court discretion based on needs and resources, not a fixed formula. Under current UK law, courts prioritise a "clean break" and mandate serious engagement with Non-Court Dispute Resolution. Payments typically cease upon the recipient's remarriage or death of either party.**

Whether you're worried about paying it or counting on receiving it, this guide will give you the hard truths — no jargon, no fluff.

## What You Need to Know

- **No automatic entitlement exists.** The court decides spousal maintenance based on individual circumstances outlined in the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, focusing on needs, income, and the lifestyle you had during the marriage — not a rigid formula.
- **Courts favour a "clean break."** UK judges prefer to sever financial ties completely wherever possible, often by converting ongoing maintenance into a one-off lump sum payment.
- **You must engage in Non-Court Dispute Resolution (NCDR) first.** Since April 2024, refusing mediation or arbitration without good reason can lead to cost penalties, even if you ultimately win in court.

## Is There a Right to Spousal Maintenance?

No. There is no automatic entitlement to spousal maintenance in England and Wales. Unlike child support (which follows a statutory formula via the Child Maintenance Service), spousal maintenance is entirely discretionary. The court looks at your situation — both of you — and makes a call.

This is governed by Section 25 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973. The judge weighs up factors like:

- The income, earning capacity, property, and other financial resources each of you has or is likely to have
- Your financial needs, obligations, and responsibilities
- The standard of living you enjoyed during the marriage
- Your ages and the length of the marriage
- Any physical or mental disability
- The contributions you each made to the family (including raising children or supporting the other's career)

It's not about punishment. It's not about fairness in the abstract. It's about need balanced against ability to pay.

## How Do Courts Decide Spousal Maintenance?

There's no calculator. No spreadsheet. The judge uses their discretion, guided by the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, to assess what's reasonable. Here's what they're thinking:

**Income and Earning Capacity:** If one spouse earns £80,000 and the other £20,000, the court considers whether the lower earner could realistically increase their income. Did they sacrifice their career to raise children? Are they older and less likely to re-enter the workforce at the same level?

**Needs vs. Wants:** The court looks at genuine financial needs — housing, utilities, food, travel, insurance — not aspirational lifestyle upgrades. But they also consider the **marital standard of living**. If you lived in a £2 million home and took annual holidays to the Maldives, the court won't ignore that reality.

**Duration of Marriage:** A 20-year marriage carries more weight than a 3-year one. Longer marriages often mean deeper financial interdependence and more justification for ongoing support.

**The Clean Break Principle:** UK courts **strongly prefer** a clean break. This means cutting financial ties completely, often by capitalising maintenance into a lump sum. For example, instead of £500/month for 10 years (£60,000 total), the paying spouse might pay £50,000 upfront, and both parties walk away.

This isn't always possible — especially if the paying spouse doesn't have liquid assets or the receiving spouse has long-term needs (e.g., caring for disabled children, health issues). But the default direction is toward independence.

## When Does Spousal Maintenance End?

Spousal maintenance automatically stops if:

- **The recipient remarries or enters a civil partnership.** The law assumes their new partner will now provide financial support.
- **Either party dies.** Maintenance is not inherited by estates.

The court can also set a **term limit** (e.g., "3 years of maintenance to allow retraining") or impose a **joint lives order** (payments continue until remarriage or death). A joint lives order is common in long marriages where one spouse has no realistic prospect of becoming self-sufficient (e.g., stayed home for 30 years, now in their 60s).

But here's the catch: even if a term is set, the court can **extend** it if circumstances change. That's why clean breaks are preferred — they provide certainty.

## What Are the Recent Changes Affecting Spousal Maintenance Cases?

Two major procedural shifts in 2024–2026 have changed how spousal maintenance cases are handled:

**The NCDR Revolution (April 2024):** Amendments to Family Procedure Rules 3.3 and 3.4 now require parties to file **Form FM5** at least 7 working days before the first hearing, detailing their views on Non-Court Dispute Resolution. The court can adjourn proceedings for NCDR without your consent, and unreasonable refusal to engage can result in cost sanctions — even if you win at trial. This isn't optional theatre. It's a mandatory checkpoint.

**The Bundle Overhaul (March 2026):** Practice Direction 27A now mandates **electronic bundles (e-bundles)** as the default for all family proceedings. These must be searchable PDFs with proper pagination, bookmarking, and a clickable index. The default page limit is 350 A4 pages — exceed it without court permission and you risk having your case removed from the list or facing adverse costs orders. The new rules strictly prohibit "unnecessary correspondence" (including general solicitor letters, social media messages, full bank statements, and photographs unless specifically directed). Position statements now have strict page limits: 6 pages for First Appointment, 15 for Final Hearing.

Non-compliance isn't a slap on the wrist. It's a derailment.

## What About Tax?

As of 2026, spousal maintenance payments in the UK are:

- **Not tax-deductible** for the payer
- **Not taxable income** for the recipient

This changed in recent years. Previously, maintenance could be treated as taxable income. Now, it's neutral — the payer doesn't get a tax break, and the recipient doesn't owe income tax on it.

This simplifies things, but it also means the paying spouse can't offset the cost. Factor this into negotiations — if you're paying £1,000/month, that's coming from your post-tax income.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I claim spousal maintenance if I'm the higher earner?**  
Yes. Gender doesn't matter. If the lower-earning spouse (regardless of sex) has a genuine need and you have the capacity to pay, maintenance may be awarded.

**What if my ex refuses to work?**  
The court considers "earning capacity," not just current income. If your ex is voluntarily unemployed or underemployed without good reason, the judge can impute a notional income based on what they *could* earn.

**Can spousal maintenance be backdated?**  
Potentially, yes — but only to the date of the application. You can't claim for years before you filed. Act quickly.

**What happens if I lose my job after the order is made?**  
You can apply to vary the order due to a material change in circumstances. But you must keep paying in the meantime — stopping without a court variation can lead to enforcement action.

**How is spousal maintenance enforced if my ex doesn't pay?**  
The court can issue an **attachment of earnings order** (deducted directly from wages), a **charging order** (secured against property), or even **committal to prison** for wilful refusal to pay.

**Is cohabitation the same as remarriage for ending maintenance?**  
No. Maintenance only automatically ends on remarriage or civil partnership. But if your ex is cohabiting with a new partner, you can apply to vary or discharge the order on the basis their financial needs have reduced.
