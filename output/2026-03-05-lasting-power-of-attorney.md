# Lasting Power of Attorney: Why Waiting Until You Need One is Too Late

Nobody plans to lose control. But here's the uncomfortable truth: an accident, a sudden illness, or a degenerative condition can strike at any age, leaving you unable to manage your own affairs. By the time you "need" a Lasting Power of Attorney, it's often too late to create one.

**A Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) is a legal document that lets you appoint trusted individuals (called attorneys) to make decisions on your behalf if you lose mental capacity. Under the Mental Capacity Act 2005, there are two types in England and Wales: one for Property and Financial Affairs, and another for Health and Welfare. Registration with the Office of the Public Guardian costs £92 per document and typically takes 8-10 weeks.** That's the technical answer. The human answer? It's your safety net—the one thing that prevents your loved ones from facing a legal nightmare when you're at your most vulnerable.

## What You Need to Know

- **Without an LPA, your family cannot automatically manage your finances or make health decisions for you**—even your spouse or children. They'd need to apply for a costly, time-consuming Court of Protection deputyship order that involves application fees and legal costs that typically exceed £2,000.
- **Two types exist:** Property and Financial Affairs (handling money, property, bills) and Health and Welfare (medical treatment, care decisions). You can set up one or both, with registration costing £92 per LPA—so £184 total if you're setting up both types.
- **LPAs must be registered with the OPG before they can be used.** This process takes 8-10 weeks if there are no errors, so don't wait until crisis hits.

## The Two Types of LPA: What Each One Does

### Property and Financial Affairs LPA

This covers everything to do with money and property: paying bills, managing bank accounts, selling your home, claiming benefits, or running a business. Here's the key point most people miss: **you can choose to activate this LPA while you still have mental capacity**. That means if you're going abroad for six months, or recovering from surgery, your attorney can step in temporarily—with your permission—to handle things for you.

Once registered, this LPA remains valid for life (or until you revoke it). If you lose capacity, your attorney automatically continues managing your finances according to your instructions.

### Health and Welfare LPA

This one only kicks in when you've lost mental capacity. It covers decisions about your daily care routine, where you live, who you see, and critically—**medical treatment decisions**, including whether to give or refuse life-sustaining treatment. You can specify preferences in the LPA itself: perhaps you want to remain at home as long as possible, or you have strong views about end-of-life care.

Your attorney must always act in your **best interests** under the Mental Capacity Act 2005, but your documented wishes carry significant weight.

## When Should You Set Up an LPA? (Spoiler: Now)

If you're over 18 and have mental capacity, the answer is simple: **now**. Here's why:

- **Loss of capacity is sudden and unpredictable.** A stroke, a car accident, early-onset dementia—these don't arrive with a warning letter. If you wait until you "need" an LPA, it's legally too late to create one.
- **The Court of Protection route is brutal.** Without an LPA, your family must apply for a deputyship order—a process that involves court application fees, ongoing supervision fees, and legal costs that typically exceed £2,000. The process takes 4-6 months minimum. Compare that to £92 per LPA registration, with fee reductions available if you're on certain benefits.
- **You're protecting your loved ones from legal limbo.** Without an LPA, your partner cannot access your joint account, your adult children cannot sell the house to pay for care, and nobody can challenge medical decisions on your behalf.

The Mental Capacity Act 2005 makes this clear in Section 1: a person must be **assumed to have capacity** unless proven otherwise. Once you lack capacity, the legal door to creating an LPA slams shut.

## The Legal Framework: Mental Capacity Act 2005

The Mental Capacity Act 2005 governs every LPA in England and Wales. It's built on five core principles that your attorneys must follow:

1. **Assume capacity unless proven otherwise.** Don't treat someone as incapable just because they make an unwise decision.
2. **Help people make their own decisions.** All practical steps must be taken before concluding someone lacks capacity.
3. **Unwise decisions don't mean incapacity.** We all make bad choices—that's not evidence of lacking capacity.
4. **Act in the person's best interests.** Any decision made on behalf of someone without capacity must prioritise their welfare, preferences, and values.
5. **Choose the least restrictive option.** Always consider whether there's a less intrusive way to achieve the same goal.

These aren't suggestions—they're legal duties. Your attorneys can't simply act on whim. They must follow your instructions, consult with family where appropriate, and keep records. The Office of the Public Guardian (OPG) has the power to investigate and remove attorneys who abuse their position.

## The Registration Process (And Why 8-10 Weeks Matters)

Creating an LPA is only half the battle. **It must be registered with the OPG before it can be used.** The current timeline is 8-10 weeks if there are no errors. Common mistakes that cause delays include:

- Incorrect signatures or witnessing (signatures must be witnessed in a specific order)
- Missing information about the donor or attorneys
- Incorrect fees or payment issues
- Incomplete "people to notify" sections

Once registered, the OPG sends you the stamped LPA document. Only then can your attorneys act. This is why we emphasise **register early**—if you wait until a crisis hits, your family could be locked out of accounts and decisions for nearly three months.

The registration fee is £92 per LPA as of 2026. If you're setting up both types (Property and Financial Affairs, and Health and Welfare), that's £184 total. Fee exemptions and reductions are available if you receive certain means-tested benefits—the OPG can advise on eligibility.

## What Powers Does Each Type of Attorney Have?

### Property and Financial Affairs Attorneys Can:

- Pay your bills and manage your bank accounts
- Collect benefits, pensions, or other income on your behalf
- Sell your home (if you've given them this power in the LPA)
- Invest your money or manage savings
- Make decisions about your property and possessions
- Run your business or deal with your tax affairs

### Health and Welfare Attorneys Can:

- Decide where you should live and with whom
- Make decisions about your daily care (washing, dressing, eating)
- Consent to or refuse medical treatment on your behalf
- Decide whether you should take part in medical research
- Make decisions about life-sustaining treatment (if you've given them this power)
- Access your medical records to make informed decisions

Both types of attorney must keep records of their decisions and, if requested, account to the OPG for how they've managed your affairs.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I make an LPA if I already have a will?**  
Yes—and you should. A will deals with what happens after you die. An LPA deals with what happens while you're still alive but unable to make decisions. They're entirely separate legal documents.

**What happens if I don't trust anyone to be my attorney?**  
You can appoint a professional attorney (such as a solicitor) or use the OPG's "replacement attorney" system. If you genuinely have nobody, the Court of Protection can appoint a deputy—but this involves significant cost and delay and should be a last resort.

**Can I change or cancel my LPA later?**  
Yes. As long as you have mental capacity, you can revoke an LPA at any time. You must notify the OPG and your attorneys in writing. If the LPA was already registered, the OPG will cancel the registration and note it on their records.

**What if my attorney doesn't act in my best interests?**  
The OPG investigates complaints about attorneys. If misconduct is proven, they can remove the attorney, apply for a court order, or even prosecute in serious cases (such as financial abuse). Anyone can raise concerns with the OPG if they believe an attorney is not acting properly.

**Do I need a solicitor to create an LPA?**  
No—you can complete the forms yourself via GOV.UK. However, most people find the process complex and benefit from legal guidance to avoid costly mistakes that delay registration. The forms must be completed in a specific order with correct witnessing, and errors can add months to the process.

**Can I appoint more than one attorney?**  
Yes. You can appoint multiple attorneys to act "jointly" (all must agree on every decision), "jointly and severally" (any one can act alone), or a mix of both for different types of decisions. Many people choose joint and several for flexibility, so decisions don't grind to a halt if one attorney is temporarily unavailable.
