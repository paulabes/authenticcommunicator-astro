## What You Need to Know

- **Court delays are severe**: Private law children cases average 36 weeks to resolve, and contested financial cases often stretch beyond a year. Going straight to court is no longer the fastest route.
- **NCDR is mandatory**: Under Family Procedure Rules 3.3 and 3.4, you must consider Non-Court Dispute Resolution (like mediation or arbitration). Refusing without good reason can result in cost penalties.
- **Digital assets are under scrutiny**: From January 2026, crypto exchanges report directly to HMRC. Hiding Bitcoin, NFTs, or online investments in divorce proceedings is financial misconduct with severe legal consequences.

## How Divorce Works in 2026: The No-Fault System

Since April 6, 2022, England and Wales have operated a no-fault divorce system. This means you no longer need to prove adultery, unreasonable behaviour, or abandonment. You simply state that the marriage has irretrievably broken down — and that's it. One person can apply (a sole application), or both can apply together (a joint application).

Here's the timeline for the divorce itself:

1. **Application**: Submit the divorce petition online or via post.
2. **20-week reflection period**: A mandatory waiting period from application to conditional order.
3. **Conditional Order**: Previously called "decree nisi" — confirms the court agrees the marriage has broken down.
4. **6-week waiting period**: From conditional to final order.
5. **Final Order**: Previously "decree absolute" — the marriage is legally dissolved.

On paper, this takes about **seven months minimum**. In reality, most divorces take longer because the ancillary matters — finances and children — drag on.

## The Real Battle: Finances and Children

The divorce decree is just admin. The real fight is over:

- **The family home**: Who stays? Who buys out whom? Do we sell?
- **Pensions**: Often the largest asset after the house, and easily overlooked.
- **Savings, investments, and debts**: Everything goes into the pot.
- **Maintenance payments**: Spousal maintenance (for you) and child maintenance (for the kids).
- **Child arrangements**: Where do the children live? How much time with each parent?

Under the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 (Section 25), the court considers multiple factors when dividing assets: the welfare of children, each party's income and needs, contributions to the marriage, and future earning capacity. Notice what's missing? Fault. Even in 2026, who cheated or who "caused" the breakdown is largely irrelevant to the financial split.

Here's the hard truth: the family court system is on its knees. As of Q2 2025, **contested financial remedy cases take over a year on average**, with complex cases stretching to 18-24 months. Private law children cases (disputes over where the child lives, contact arrangements) average **36 weeks to disposal**. Over 4,000 children are stuck in cases lasting 100+ weeks or more.

If you and your ex can't agree, you're not getting a quick court date. You're joining a queue that moves at a glacial pace — and costs a fortune. Traditional solicitors charge by the hour, meaning every letter, every court hearing, every delay is another line on your bill.

## The NCDR Revolution: Mediation, Arbitration, and the Courts Are Watching

In April 2024, the Family Procedure Rules changed the game. The court now **mandates that you consider Non-Court Dispute Resolution (NCDR)** at every stage of proceedings. This includes mediation, arbitration, early neutral evaluation, and collaborative law.

Here's what that means in practice:

- **You must file Form FM5**: This form outlines whether you've considered NCDR and why you think it is or isn't appropriate.
- **The court can adjourn your case**: Even if you want to charge ahead, the judge can pause proceedings to give you time to try mediation or arbitration.
- **Refusal has consequences**: If you unreasonably refuse NCDR, the court can order you to pay the other side's legal costs. Recent cases like *AM v RF* (2024) show that judges are serious about this.

## Digital Assets: The New Frontier (and a Hiding Place for the Dishonest)

If you or your ex hold cryptocurrency, NFTs, online trading accounts, or even digital art in the metaverse, listen carefully: **these are matrimonial assets, and you must disclose them**.

From January 1, 2026, the UK's implementation of the Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework (CARF) means that UK-based crypto exchanges and wallet providers report your holdings directly to HMRC. HMRC treats crypto as property, not currency, which means gains are subject to Capital Gains Tax — and in divorce, it's part of the pot to be divided.

Hiding crypto is financial misconduct. If undisclosed assets are later discovered, the court can:

- **Draw adverse inferences** (assume you have more hidden).
- **Adjust the settlement** heavily in your ex's favour.
- **Impose costs orders** against you.
- **Set aside the final order** and reopen the case.
- **Initiate contempt proceedings** in extreme cases.

Forensic accountants can trace blockchain transactions. Courts can issue freezing injunctions. The idea that crypto is "untraceable" is a dangerous myth in 2026.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I have to go to court to get divorced?**
No. The divorce application itself is administrative and doesn't require a court hearing. Financial and child arrangement disputes may end up in court if you can't agree through NCDR.

**How long does a divorce take in 2026?**
The divorce decree itself takes a minimum of about seven months (20-week reflection period plus 6-week wait for final order). Resolving finances and children arrangements can take much longer — often over a year if contested.

**What is Non-Court Dispute Resolution (NCDR)?**
NCDR includes mediation, arbitration, collaborative law, and early neutral evaluation. It's a way to resolve disputes outside the courtroom, and from April 2024, the court mandates you consider it. Refusing without good reason can lead to cost penalties.

**Do I have to declare my cryptocurrency in a divorce?**
Yes. Digital assets are property under UK law and must be disclosed on Form E. Hiding crypto is financial misconduct and can result in severe penalties, including adverse financial adjustments and costs orders.

**Can I do a DIY divorce?**
You can apply for the divorce decree yourself, but resolving finances and children arrangements without legal advice is risky. Inadequate settlements, overlooked pensions, or non-compliance with NCDR rules can cost you far more in the long run.

**What happens to the family home?**
It depends on your specific circumstances: needs of any children, financial resources, contributions, and future earning capacity. Options include one party buying out the other, selling and splitting proceeds, or a "Mesher order" (sale deferred until children are older).
