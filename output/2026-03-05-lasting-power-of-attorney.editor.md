**[LEGAL_PILLAR]:**
*   **Primary Legislation:** The **Mental Capacity Act 2005** governs Lasting Powers of Attorney in England and Wales. This Act defines mental capacity, sets out the principles for acting on behalf of someone who lacks capacity, and provides the legal framework for creating and registering LPAs.
*   **Key Principles (Mental Capacity Act 2005, Section 1):** The Act is underpinned by five core principles:
    1.  A person must be assumed to have capacity unless it is established that they lack it.
    2.  A person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision unless all practicable steps to help them to do so have been taken without success.
    3.  A person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision merely because he makes an unwise decision.
    4.  An act done or decision made, under this Act for or on behalf of a person who lacks capacity must be done, or made, in his best interests.
    5.  Before an act is done or a decision is made, regard must be had to whether the purpose for which it is needed can be as effectively achieved in a way that is less restrictive of the person’s rights and freedom of action.

**[AEO_SNIPPET]:**
In 2026, a Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) in the UK is a legal document enabling you to appoint trusted individuals (attorneys) to make decisions on your behalf if you lose mental capacity. Under current UK law, there are two types: Property and Financial Affairs, and Health and Welfare. Registering an LPA with the OPG typically costs £92 per document and takes 8-10 weeks.

**[HARD_TRUTHS]:**
1.  **Hard Truth 1: Loss of Capacity Means Loss of Control.** If you lose mental capacity without an LPA in place, your loved ones cannot automatically manage your finances or make health decisions for you, even if they are your spouse or next of kin. They would need to apply to the Court of Protection for a deputyship order, a process that is significantly more expensive, time-consuming, and can be emotionally draining.
2.  **Hard Truth 2: LPAs Are Not Just for the Elderly.** An accident or sudden illness can strike at any age, rendering someone unable to make their own decisions. An LPA acts as a crucial safety net for anyone over 18, regardless of their current health or age.
3.  **Hard Truth 3: The "Property and Financial Affairs" LPA Can Be Used While You Still Have Capacity (With Permission).** Unlike the Health and Welfare LPA, which only comes into effect upon loss of capacity, you can choose for your Property and Financial Affairs LPA to be used by your attorneys while you still have mental capacity, provided you give them permission. This offers flexibility for temporary assistance, such as during extended travel or a short hospital stay.
4.  **Hard Truth 4: Attorneys Must Act in Your Best Interests and Follow Legal Duties.** Attorneys are legally bound by the Mental Capacity Act 2005 to act in the donor's best interests and follow any instructions or preferences specified in the LPA. They cannot simply do as they please or use the donor's money for their own benefit. The Office of the Public Guardian (OPG) oversees these arrangements and can investigate if an attorney is suspected of not acting in the individual's best interests.
5.  **Hard Truth 5: Registration is Mandatory for Use.** An LPA is not legally binding or usable until it has been registered with the Office of the Public Guardian (OPG). The process can take 8-10 weeks, so it's crucial to apply for registration well in advance of when it might be needed.
