# Pension Sharing in Divorce: What You Need to Know in 2026

Your pension is often your largest asset after the family home. When divorce lands, the question of who gets what from retirement savings can feel overwhelming — especially when you're already dealing with the emotional weight of separation. You're not alone, and you're in the right place.

**Pension sharing is one of three ways to divide retirement assets in divorce. It allows the court to award a percentage of one spouse's pension directly to the other, creating a clean financial break. This differs from pension offsetting (trading pension value for other assets) and pension attachment (receiving payments only when the pension is drawn). Under the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, as amended by the Welfare Reform and Pensions Act 1999, pensions are treated as matrimonial assets and must be disclosed during financial remedy proceedings.**

The truth is, most people underestimate how much their pensions are worth — and how complicated it gets when you're trying to split them fairly.

## What You Need to Know

- **Pensions must be disclosed:** Every pension you hold — workplace, private, state — is part of the financial picture. Hiding pension assets is a serious breach that can derail your settlement and potentially lead to the court setting aside any financial order.
- **Three methods exist:** Pension sharing, pension offsetting, and pension attachment each work differently. Choosing the wrong one can cost you financially for decades.
- **Court delays are real:** With fewer family court sitting days from 2026 onwards, financial remedy hearings are taking longer. The average divorce now takes 74 weeks from application to final order (up 7 weeks from Q1 2024), with sole applications averaging 60 weeks to final order and joint applications 50 weeks to final order.

## Why Pensions Are Often the Forgotten Asset

When you think "divorce," you think house, savings, maybe the car. Pensions? They feel distant. Untouchable. Something for "Future You" to worry about.

But here's the reality: **pensions are deferred income**. They're money you've already earned but haven't accessed yet. The law treats them exactly like the equity in your home or the balance in your ISA.

Under Section 25 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, courts must consider "the value to each of the parties to the marriage of any benefit which... that party will lose the chance of acquiring." That's legal speak for: if you're giving up a claim on your spouse's pension, the court needs to account for that loss.

The Welfare Reform and Pensions Act 1999 introduced pension sharing orders specifically to allow a clean break. Before 1999, you couldn't split the pension itself — you could only offset it or attach future payments. Now, you can walk away with your own pension pot, independent of your ex.

## The Three Ways to Handle Pensions in Divorce

Not all pension solutions are created equal. Here's how they differ:

### Pension Sharing

The court awards a percentage of one spouse's pension to the other. A formal Pension Sharing Order is made under Section 24B of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 (as inserted by Section 19 of the Welfare Reform and Pensions Act 1999). The receiving spouse gets their own pension pot, completely separate from their ex. This is the cleanest break — you're not waiting for your ex to retire to access your share.

**Example:** If one spouse has a £200,000 pension and the court orders a 50% share, the receiving spouse gets a £100,000 pension in their own name.

**How it works:** The Pension Sharing Order specifies a percentage (not a fixed amount) to be transferred. The pension provider then implements this by either creating an internal credit within the same scheme or transferring the value to an external pension arrangement. Under the Welfare Reform and Pensions Act 1999, the pension provider has four months from the order becoming effective to implement the share.

### Pension Offsetting

You trade pension value for other assets. One spouse keeps their full pension, while the other receives more of the house equity, savings, or other property. This is done through the court's general powers under Section 23 and Section 24 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 to make lump sum orders and property adjustment orders — not through a specific "offsetting order."

**Example:** One spouse keeps a £200,000 pension, while the other gets an extra £100,000 in house equity instead.

**The risk:** Pensions grow tax-free. Cash and property don't. You might think you're getting "equal value," but in 20 years, the pension could be worth far more. Additionally, pension income has different tax treatment than capital, which can significantly affect the long-term fairness of the offset.

### Pension Attachment (Earmarking)

The receiving spouse gets a share of pension payments only when the pension-holding spouse retires. This doesn't create a separate pot — it's an ongoing claim against future income, made under Section 25B of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 (inserted by the Pensions Act 1995).

**The problem:** If your ex remarries, dies, or changes their retirement plans, your entitlement can vanish or be significantly affected. This method is rarely recommended in 2026 because it keeps you financially tied to your ex indefinitely. There's no clean break.

## How Pensions Are Valued: The CETV

To divide a pension, you first need to know what it's worth. This is called the **Cash Equivalent Transfer Value (CETV)** — a lump sum figure representing what the pension would be worth if transferred today.

Every pension provider must provide a CETV on request under the Pension Schemes Act 1993. For Defined Contribution pensions (like most modern workplace pensions), this is straightforward — the CETV usually reflects the actual fund value. For Defined Benefit pensions (like NHS, civil service, or older final salary schemes), it's more complex.

**Warning:** A CETV for a Defined Benefit pension can significantly undervalue or overvalue the true benefit depending on individual circumstances. A £30,000-per-year guaranteed pension for life might show a CETV of £500,000 — but the actual long-term value to you could be far higher or lower depending on your age, health, and life expectancy. This is where actuarial reports become essential.

The Pensions Advisory Group guidance (PAG2) recommends specialist pension valuations in cases involving Defined Benefit schemes, especially where there's a significant age gap between spouses, where one spouse has a much larger pension, or where there are health considerations that might affect life expectancy.

## Which Pensions Are Included?

All pensions are on the table:

- **Workplace pensions** (Defined Contribution and Defined Benefit)
- **Private pensions and SIPPs**
- **State Pension** (can be considered in offsetting but cannot be subject to a Pension Sharing Order)
- **Overseas pensions** (if you or your spouse worked abroad — though implementation can be complex)

You cannot hide pensions. Full financial disclosure is mandatory under Form E requirements (Form E being the standard financial statement required in financial remedy proceedings under the Family Procedure Rules 2010). If you fail to disclose a pension — even one you've "forgotten" about — the court can set aside any financial order and order you to pay costs.

## Common Questions About Pension Sharing

**Do I automatically get half of my spouse's pension?**

No. Courts aim for fairness, not automatic 50/50 splits. Section 25 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 requires courts to consider the length of the marriage, each spouse's financial needs and resources, age, contributions to the marriage (including non-financial contributions like childcare), and the standard of living during the marriage. The starting point may be equality, but the outcome depends on all these factors.

**What if my spouse has multiple pensions?**

All pensions must be disclosed and valued. You may choose to share one large pension, share multiple pensions, or offset smaller ones against other assets. The key is ensuring full disclosure on Form E and obtaining accurate CETVs for all schemes.

**Can I share a pension if we're not yet divorced?**

No. A Pension Sharing Order can only take effect once the divorce is finalised (after the Final Order is granted). This has been the law since pension sharing was introduced in 1999, and remains the case under the no-fault divorce system introduced by the Divorce, Dissolution and Separation Act 2020, which came into force in April 2022.

**What happens to my State Pension?**

The State Pension cannot be shared via a Pension Sharing Order. However, it can be considered in the overall financial settlement and offsetting calculations. Additionally, under State Pension substitution rules, if you built up entitlement to a basic State Pension based on your spouse's National Insurance contributions before 6 April 2016, you may retain some entitlement post-divorce depending on when you reach State Pension age and the specifics of your contribution record.

**How long does a Pension Sharing Order take to implement?**

Once the court issues the Pension Sharing Order and it becomes effective (usually after the Final Order is granted), the pension provider has four months to implement the pension share and transfer the funds under Section 34 of the Welfare Reform and Pensions Act 1999. However, this timeline can vary if there are complications with the scheme or if the receiving party needs to open a new pension arrangement.

**Can a Pension Sharing Order be changed later?**

No. Once a Pension Sharing Order is finalised and implemented, it is permanent and cannot be varied. This is fundamentally different from other financial orders (like periodical payments) which can sometimes be varied. This is why getting expert advice upfront is critical — you only get one chance to get this right.
